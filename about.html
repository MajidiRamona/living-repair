Why heritage belongs in the climate conversation.

A short framing — for delegates, journalists, and curious passers-by — built on Chiara Bortolotto's Will Heritage Save Us? (Cambridge University Press, 2025) and twenty years of UNESCO ICH practice.

The shift

For most of the twentieth century, heritage was a thing we tried to protect. Buildings, manuscripts, monuments — fragile artefacts that needed funding, fences, and conservators. The ambition was noble but past-facing: salvage what was vanishing.

Since the 2003 UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage came into force, that framing has quietly inverted. The Convention recognised that some forms of heritage — songs, rituals, repair, cooking, weather knowledge, water management — only exist when people do them. They are constantly recreated. They are alive.

And once heritage is alive, it has agency. It can adapt. It can solve problems. It can save us — instead of waiting to be saved.

Why this is a COP issue

Climate negotiations have, until recently, treated culture as a soft side-event — a folkloric backdrop to the harder business of emissions and finance. That framing is breaking down. The 2023 Global Stocktake explicitly mentioned indigenous and local knowledge. UNESCO's 2022 MONDIACULT declaration called culture a "global public good." The 2030 Agenda's seventeen Sustainable Development Goals have, since 2016, been folded directly into the operational directives of the ICH Convention.

The reason is practical. A water tribunal that has equitably resolved drought disputes for a thousand years is climate adaptation infrastructure. A repair workshop that diverts a tonne of textiles from landfill is mitigation. A festival that pulls a flooded city back together after a hurricane is resilience. None of these things appear on the standard NDC spreadsheet, but all of them are doing the work.

Six fields of intersection

UNESCO's own 2013 framing identified six areas where intangible heritage and sustainability concretely meet. We organise the dashboard around them:

  1. Food security — indigenous foodways, seed-saving, traditional agriculture.
  2. Social cohesion — festivals, communal rituals, peace-building practices.
  3. Sustainable environment — local ecological knowledge, land and forest stewardship.
  4. Health — traditional medicine, mental health rituals, public-health communication via culture.
  5. Livelihoods — craftsmanship, slow fashion, repair economies.
  6. Dispute resolution — customary courts, joking relationships, restorative practices.

Climate cases

Three concrete examples drawn from the Convention record stand out for COP 31:

Avalanche Risk Management (Switzerland & Austria, inscribed 2018) — Alpine empirical knowledge of snow, terrain, and risk, formally combined with scientific monitoring. A working template for what climate-adaptive ICH looks like as the snowpack destabilises.

The Water Tribunals of Valencia and Murcia — customary courts that have allocated scarce irrigation water for over a millennium. Their verdicts are recognised in Spanish civil law. As Mediterranean Europe enters chronic drought, the tribunals are a live institution, not a heritage display.

Joumou soup and borscht cooking — both inscribed under emergency procedure (2021, 2022). Foodways are not merely symbolic; they are how a community holds itself together when supply chains, governments, and infrastructure all fail at once.

Why Geneva

Geneva sits at the intersection of UN climate diplomacy, the Red Cross, the World Meteorological Organization, and a dense civil-society network. It is also home to small but precise heritage initiatives — Histoires Sans Chute recording oral memory, Amak-bro running a repair workshop — that are doing tangible climate work without ever calling themselves "climate" projects.

Geneva is the right host for a small platform that argues for taking that quiet work seriously.

Cautions

Three honest caveats. First, "heritage" can be weaponised — to exclude, to nationalise, to romanticise unequal practices. The Convention itself includes a human-rights compatibility clause for that reason, and we apply the same filter to submissions.

Second, not all intangible heritage is sustainable. Some traditions involve unsustainable extraction, animal harm, or social hierarchy. We do not list those, even when they appear on UNESCO's lists.

Third, "sustainabilization" — Bortolotto's term — risks turning living practice into yet another bureaucratic indicator. The dashboard is offered carefully: as a way to give communities credit for work they were already doing, not as a way to flatten that work into a metric.
