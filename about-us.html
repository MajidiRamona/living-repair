About us · Geneva · 2026

A student platform for COP 31.

Living Repair was designed in 2026 by students of the University of Geneva, in the seminar Heritage for Sustainable Development, as a small contribution to the COP 31 conversation hosted in our city.

Who we are

We are Master's students at the University of Geneva (UNIGE), working in 2026 within the seminar Heritage for Sustainable Development. The course asks a question that does not yet have a settled answer: what does cultural heritage actually do, in concrete terms, for the climate and sustainability agenda?

Our backgrounds are mixed — international relations, environmental studies, anthropology, art history. None of us are professional web developers; this site is a coursework artefact, not a finished product. We made it because writing essays about heritage and climate felt insufficient when COP 31 was being hosted across town.

Why we built it

The 2003 UNESCO Convention on the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage has, in the last decade, quietly become an environmental instrument. Inscriptions explicitly tied to climate adaptation went from a handful in 2010 to dozens in 2024. But that shift is mostly invisible to climate negotiators, and even to most of the heritage sector itself.

We wanted a small, public-facing object that makes the link visible. Not a database, not a policy brief — a platform that can be skimmed in five minutes by a delegate, a journalist, or anyone curious about why a repair workshop in Geneva belongs in the same conversation as a national emissions inventory.

How we worked

We chose to feature only two initiatives at launch — Histoires Sans Chute and Amak-bro, both based here in Geneva. We did this on purpose. A long catalogue of every interesting cultural project on Earth would have been easy to assemble and easy to ignore. Two well-described local cases, paired with the broader UNESCO data on the dashboard, force the reader to take the connection seriously.

The dashboard data is drawn from UNESCO's public lists and the operational record of the 2003 Convention. The featured initiatives were profiled with the cooperation of the organizations themselves; we are grateful for their patience with our questions and our drafts.

The platform is intentionally light: static HTML, vanilla JavaScript, no tracking, no accounts. It can be hosted from a folder. We hope this means it can outlive our seminar.

Thanks

To the seminar instructor and teaching team for the framing and the freedom to take it in this direction. To Histoires Sans Chute and Amak-bro for letting us write about their work. To everyone in the Geneva heritage and climate communities who answered an email from a graduate student they had never heard of.

Any errors of fact or framing on this site are ours alone, and we welcome corrections.
